## Survival Skills Guide: Cooking Fires

This guide provides essential knowledge for safely and effectively building cooking fires in wilderness survival and emergency preparedness scenarios. Mastering this skill is crucial for water purification, food preparation, warmth, and morale in challenging conditions. Always prioritize safety, adhere to local regulations, and practice responsible wilderness stewardship.

### Materials/Equipment Needed

Successful fire-starting relies on having the right materials and tools. Always gather more than you think you'll need.

*   **Ignition Source:**
    *   Primary: Waterproof matches, disposable lighter (check fuel), ferrocerium rod with striker.
    *   Alternatives: Magnifying glass (sunny conditions), battery with steel wool, char cloth.
*   **Tinder (highly flammable, catches spark easily):**
    *   Natural: Dry grass, birch bark shavings, pine needles, cottonwood fluff, fine wood shavings.
    *   Man-made: Cotton balls (petroleum jelly soaked is excellent), dryer lint, char cloth.
*   **Kindling (small twigs, pencil-lead to pencil-thickness):**
    *   Dry twigs, small split wood, bark strips.
*   **Fuelwood (larger branches, wrist to forearm thickness):**
    *   Dry, dead standing wood or fallen branches (avoid green wood, it smokes and burns poorly).
*   **Fire Pit Construction/Maintenance:**
    *   Small shovel or trowel, sturdy rocks for a fire ring.
*   **Safety Equipment:**
    *   Water container (bucket, large bottle) filled with water.
    *   Small shovel/trowel for fire suppression and site preparation.
    *   Heavy-duty gloves (for handling hot materials or sharp wood).
    *   Basic first-aid kit (especially for burns).
*   **Cooking Gear (Optional but Recommended):**
    *   Metal pot or pan, sturdy cooking grate or improvised trivet.

### Step-by-Step Instructions

Follow these steps carefully to build a safe and effective cooking fire.

1.  **Site Selection and Preparation:**
    *   **Procedure:** Choose a level, clear area away from overhanging branches, dry grass, tents, and other flammable materials. Ideally, use an existing fire ring. If not, clear a 10-foot (3-meter) radius down to bare mineral soil. Dig a shallow fire pit (4-6 inches deep, 18-24 inches wide) and line it with rocks to contain the fire and radiate heat.
    *   **Safety Warning:** Never build a fire under low-hanging branches or near dense, dry vegetation. Ensure sufficient clearance (at least 10 feet) around your fire pit to prevent accidental spread. Have your water source and shovel immediately accessible.
