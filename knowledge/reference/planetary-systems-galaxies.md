## Overview

Understanding planetary systems and galactic structures provides perspective on our place in the cosmos and enhances night sky navigation skills. This guide covers structure, scales, composition, and observable features of planets, star systems, and galaxies.

## Planet Classification & Characteristics

### Inner Planets (Terrestrial/Rocky)

```
Interior Structure:
    Crust (thin)
    └─ Mantle (silicate rock)
       └─ Core (iron-nickel)

Characteristics:
✓ Solid surface
✓ Thin/no atmosphere (except Venus)
✓ Few or no moons
✓ Slow rotation
✓ Higher density
✓ Dense core
```

### Outer Planets (Gas/Ice Giants)

```
Composition (No solid surface):

Jupiter/Saturn (Gas Giants):
    Hydrogen atmosphere
    └─ Helium clouds
       └─ Liquid hydrogen/water layer
          └─ Rock/metal core (small)

Uranus/Neptune (Ice Giants):
    Ice = water, methane, ammonia
    (NOT frozen solid, hot dense fluid)
    
Characteristics:
✓ No solid surface
✓ Thick atmosphere
✓ Many moons (10+)
✓ Rapid rotation
✓ Lower density (float if placed in water!)
✓ Ring systems
```

## Detection Methods

### How We Know About Exoplanets & Distant Galaxies

| Method | Detection | Used For | Accuracy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Transit** | Star dims as planet crosses | Exoplanet discovery | High (size/orbit) |
| **Radial Velocity** | Star wobbles toward/away | Exoplanet mass | Medium (mass/orbit) |
| **Direct Imaging** | Actual light from planet | Young hot planets | Low (young planets) |
| **Gravitational Lensing** | Light bends around object | Distant galaxies, black holes | Very high |
| **Redshift** | Light wavelength shift | Galaxy distance, age | Medium (distance estimate) |
| **Microlensing** | Star magnifies background star | Exoplanet masses | Low (small planets) |

## Practice: Identifying Planetary Motions

### Exercise 1: Track a Visible Planet

**Goal:** Observe a planet over several nights and see its motion

**Difficulty:** Beginner  
**Time:** 15 minutes × 5 nights  
**Equipment:** Star chart, location, binoculars optional

**Procedure:**
1. Identify bright planet visible (Jupiter, Venus, Mars, or Saturn)
2. Note its position relative to stars on night 1
3. Repeat on nights 3, 5, 7, 10
4. Plot motion on star chart
5. Calculate angular speed

**Success:** Visible motion detected; can predict future position

### Exercise 2: Estimate Galaxy Distance

**Goal:** Use observable properties to estimate distance

**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Time:** 1-2 hours  
**Equipment:** Telescope, star chart, calculator

**Procedure:**
1. Observe nearby galaxy (M31, M33, M51)
2. Estimate apparent brightness
3. Research known absolute brightness
4. Calculate distance using brightness formula
5. Compare to published distance

**Success:** Distance estimate within 20% of true value
