# Heat Exhaustion: Survival Guide

## Overview
Heat exhaustion is a serious condition that occurs when your body overheats due to prolonged exposure to high temperatures, often accompanied by dehydration. It's a precursor to heat stroke, a life-threatening emergency, making prompt recognition and treatment crucial for survival in hot environments. Recognizing the signs and taking immediate action can prevent the condition from escalating.

## Materials Needed
*   **Potable Water:** At least 2-3 liters; more is better.
*   **Electrolyte Solution:** Sports drink, oral rehydration salts (ORS), or ingredients to make your own (salt, sugar).
*   **Cooling Cloths/Rags:** Bandanas, towels, or any absorbent fabric.
*   **Fan (optional):** A small battery-powered fan or a hand fan made from cardboard/leaves.
*   **Shade/Shelter:** Natural shade (trees, rock overhangs) or a makeshift tarp/tent.
*   **Thermometer (optional):** For monitoring body temperature if available.
*   **Basic First-Aid Kit:** For any minor injuries or discomfort.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1.  **Recognize the Symptoms:** Be alert for the signs in yourself or others. Key indicators include heavy sweating, pale and clammy skin, muscle cramps (especially in legs, arms, or abdomen), fatigue, weakness, dizziness, headache, nausea, vomiting, and a rapid, weak pulse. The person may also feel faint or irritable.
2.  **Move to a Cool Place Immediately:** The most critical first step. Get out of direct sunlight and into the coolest available shade. If indoors, move to an air-conditioned or cooler room. If no natural shade is available, construct a makeshift shelter using a tarp, blanket, or even large leaves.
3.  **Loosen and Remove Clothing:** Remove any unnecessary layers of clothing. Loosen tight garments around the neck, waist, and chest to allow for better air circulation and cooling. If possible, change into light, breathable clothing.
4.  **Hydrate with Electrolytes:** Begin sipping cool (not ice-cold) water or an electrolyte solution. Encourage small, frequent sips rather than large gulps, especially if nausea is present. Aim for 4-8 ounces (120-240 ml) every 15-20 minutes. Avoid alcohol, caffeine, and sugary sodas, as these can worsen dehydration.
5.  **Actively Cool the Body:**
    *   **Wet Compresses:** Soak cloths or rags in cool water and apply them to the skin. Focus on pulse points where blood vessels are close to the surface: the forehead, neck, armpits, and groin.
    *   **Fanning:** Fan the person vigorously to promote evaporative cooling. If a fan isn't available, use a piece of cardboard, a large leaf, or your hand.
    *   **Spraying:** Gently mist the skin with water if a spray bottle is available.
    *   **Foot Soaking (optional):** If practical, immerse feet in cool water.
6.  **Elevate Legs:** If the person feels faint or dizzy, have them lie down with their legs elevated slightly (6-12 inches) above heart level. This helps improve blood flow to the brain.
