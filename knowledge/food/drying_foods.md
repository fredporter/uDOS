## Overview

# Drying Foods

Drying is one of the oldest and most effective methods of food preservation, crucial for survival situations where refrigeration is unavailable. By removing moisture, you prevent the growth of bacteria, yeasts, and molds, significantly extending the shelf life and portability of food items like fruits, vegetables, and meat. Mastering this technique ensures a lightweight, nutrient-dense food supply for your long-term needs.

## Materials Needed

*   **Food to be dried:** Fruits (apples, berries), vegetables (carrots, leafy greens), lean meats (venison, fish).
*   **Sharp knife and cutting board:** For preparation and uniform slicing.
*   **Drying racks/screens:** Can be purpose-built or improvised from clean sticks, netting, old window screens (ensure no lead paint), or clean cloth stretched over a frame. Ensure good airflow.
*   **Clean cloth or paper towels:** For patting food dry.
*   **Covering (optional but recommended):** Fine mesh netting, cheesecloth, or thin fabric to protect food from insects and dust during drying.
*   **Heat source:** Direct sun, consistent breeze, or low, controlled heat from a fire.
*   **Airtight storage containers:** Glass jars, sealed bags, or repurposed containers for storing dried food.
*   **Optional:** Lemon juice or citric acid (for fruits to prevent browning), salt (for meat preservation).

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1.  **Prepare the Food:**
    *   **Fruits & Vegetables:** Wash thoroughly. Peel if desired (some nutrients are in the skin). Remove cores, seeds, or tough stems. Slice uniformly (1/8 to 1/4 inch thick) to ensure even drying. For vegetables like carrots or potatoes, blanching (briefly boiling then chilling) can help retain color and nutrients. For fruits prone to browning (apples), dip slices in a mixture of lemon juice and water (1:1 ratio) for 5 minutes, then pat dry.
    *   **Meats:** Choose lean cuts. Trim all visible fat, as fat can go rancid. Slice meat uniformly against the grain into thin strips (1/8 to 1/4 inch thick). Marinating in salt, spices, or a soy-based mixture can add flavor and aid preservation, but ensure it's not too moist.
